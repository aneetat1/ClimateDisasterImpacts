Essential Information

Our research focuses primarily on climate trends in Texas and Australia. Our decision to investigate Texas was a suggestion by our mentor based on the location of the internship (Austin, TX). Recent trends in the Texas climate suggested conditions similar to the 2010-2012 La Niña event, where wildfires broke out across the state due to dry conditions. Because of this, the majority of our research focuses on La Niña induced climate disasters like wildfires, hurricanes, and flooding.

We also decided to investigate Australia since its proximity to the equator is similar to Texas, but it typically experiences the opposite climate at any given time. When it is dry in Texas, there is typically more precipitation in Australia, and vice versa. Additionally, because they are separated by the Pacific, they are inversely affected by El Nino and La Nina. Investigating trends across both Texas and Australia can give us insight into how certain climate trends impact different locations on a global scale.

These two maps represent the anomaly in ocean surface temperature leading to an El Nino or La Nina event. Each calculate an average of ocean surface temperatures over 10 years leading to the event, then find the difference between that and the event year, forming a visual representation of the difference from average conditions.
